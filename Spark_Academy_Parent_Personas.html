Persona 1: Sarah — The Intentional First-Timer

"I'm not looking for daycare. I'm looking for the right foundation for my daughter."

Quick Profile

Age31
LocationMorton, IL
HouseholdMarried; husband works full-time in ag/manufacturing. Sarah is stay-at-home mom.
EducationBachelor's degree (Elementary Education). Husband has a bachelor's in engineering.
IncomeHousehold $95K–$130K
ChildrenOne daughter, turning 3 in September
EthnicityWhite (representative of the majority demographic in Morton)
Entry Point3's Preschool (T/Th at $350/mo)
Discovery SourceFacebook / Instagram, then website

Who Sarah Is

Sarah is deliberate about everything involving her daughter. She left her teaching career when her daughter was born and plans to stay home through the preschool years. She has a background in education herself — which means she evaluates preschools with a more informed eye than the average parent. She knows the difference between a structured play environment and a glorified playroom. She's not impressed by cute decorations or catchy taglines. She wants to understand the curriculum, meet the teacher, and know the philosophy before she'll even schedule a tour.

She found Spark on a local mom's Facebook group. Another parent mentioned it in a thread about preschools in Morton, and Sarah clicked through to the Instagram page first. The photos looked intentional — not staged, but clearly showing children engaged in real learning activities. She then went to the website to learn more.

Her husband will attend the tour with her and has veto power, but Sarah drives the research. She's the one comparing options in a spreadsheet at 10pm. Her husband's main concern is whether the investment is worth it compared to waiting another year.

What Sarah Is Looking For

  • A school that takes early childhood education as seriously as she does — not a place that treats preschool as babysitting with crafts
  • A credentialed, experienced leader who can articulate the "why" behind the curriculum — not just the "what"
  • Evidence that her daughter will be genuinely known, not just supervised. Small class sizes, consistent teachers, individualized attention.
  • A clear daily structure that balances play, learning, and socialization — she wants to see the actual schedule
  • A school she can feel proud to name when other parents ask "where does your daughter go?"

Sarah's Dominant Emotional Triggers

Aspiration"I want my daughter to be confident, curious, and socially ready for kindergarten — not just able to count to 20."

What Converts Sarah

  • Tour page should frame the visit as a conversation, not an evaluation: "We'd love to show you what a day at Spark looks like"
  • Avoid any language that sounds like daycare, childcare, or drop-off convenience. Sarah will bounce immediately.

Persona 2: David & Priya — The ROI-Conscious Dual Evaluators

"We can afford this. What I need to know is whether it's worth it compared to the other options."

Quick Profile

AgesDavid is 38, Priya is 36
LocationWashington, IL (15-minute drive to Morton)
HouseholdMarried; David is a financial analyst. Priya is stay-at-home after a career in healthcare administration.
EducationDavid has an MBA. Priya has a master's in health administration.
IncomeHousehold $140K–$180K
ChildrenSon, age 4 (currently in a part-time church program). Daughter, age 18 months.
EthnicitySouth Asian / White (mixed-ethnicity household)
Entry Point4's Preschool (M/W/F at $450/mo) or Kindergarten Prep
Discovery SourceFriend's recommendation, then Google research to verify

Who David & Priya Are

David and Priya are the family that does not make impulse decisions. They're college-educated, analytically minded, and approach preschool selection the same way they approached buying their house — with research, comparisons, and a clear rubric for what "good" looks like.

Their son has been in a church-based preschool two mornings a week since age 3. It's been fine — safe, friendly, affordable. But Priya has started noticing that her son isn't being challenged. He's bored during free play. He's not developing the pre-reading skills she expected by now. A friend whose daughter attends Spark mentioned how much her child had grown in just one semester, and that sparked the conversation.

Priya does the initial research. She'll find 3–4 schools, read every page of their websites, and create a shortlist. David joins for the tours and applies his analytical lens: What's the student-teacher ratio? What are the actual outcomes? How does the cost compare per hour, per day, per developmental outcome? He's not cheap — he's value-conscious. He'll pay more if the ROI is clear.

The family is willing to drive from Washington to Morton for the right school. Geography is not the deciding factor — quality is. But they are also comparing Spark against full-day programs, because Priya sometimes wonders if a longer day would be better for their son's development (and would give her more time with their toddler).

What David & Priya Are Looking For

  • Tangible evidence that Spark produces better outcomes than their current church-based program — not just different vibes, but measurable differences
  • A clear articulation of what their son will learn, achieve, and be ready for by the time he enters kindergarten
  • Credentials that justify the tuition premium. The fact that the curriculum is designed by someone with a doctorate matters to David because it signals rigor. It matters to Priya because it signals the school takes child development seriously.
  • Transparency on tuition, schedule, and what's included. No surprises. No hidden fees.
  • A school that reflects their values: diversity, inclusivity, respect for every child's pace of development

David & Priya's Dominant Emotional Triggers

TriggerWhat It Sounds Like in Their Heads
Fear of Falling Behind (Primary)"Our son starts kindergarten in a year. If he's not ready — socially, academically, emotionally — that's on us. We can't get this year back."
Aspiration"We want him to walk into kindergarten confident. Not anxious, not behind, not clinging to us at drop-off."
Trust"A doctorate in education? That's not something you see at the average preschool. That matters."

  • The neural pathways argument belongs on the cost/value page or in an FAQ: "The bulk of brain pathways are formed before kindergarten. Preschool is the highest-ROI investment you can make in your child's future."
  • Blog content targeting "half day vs full day preschool," "is preschool worth the cost," and "kindergarten readiness checklist" will capture David and Priya at the research stage
  • Location-based landing pages for Washington, IL and East Peoria are essential — this family is driving in from outside Morton and is searching with those geographic qualifiers
  • The tour page should mention that both parents are welcome and encouraged to attend. David is part of the decision — the website should speak to him too.

Persona 3: Megan — The Values-First Comparer

"I want a school that sees my son as a whole person — not just a kid who needs to learn his letters."

Quick Profile

Age34
LocationEast Peoria, IL
HouseholdMarried; husband is a physical therapist. Megan works part-time remotely in nonprofit communications (10–15 hrs/week during naps).
EducationMegan has a master's in social work. Husband has a doctorate in physical therapy (DPT).
IncomeHousehold $110K–$145K
ChildrenSon, age 3 (late August birthday — just turned 3). Considering a second child.
EthnicityBlack / multiracial household
Entry Point3's Preschool (T/Th at $350/mo), potentially adding Fresh 3's Fridays
Discovery SourceInstagram ad → Instagram page → Website

Who Megan Is

Megan is the parent who leads with values. She's not comparing preschools on a spreadsheet — she's evaluating them on whether the school's philosophy aligns with how she's raising her son at home. She believes childhood is sacred and that three-year-olds should not be drilled on worksheets. But she also knows that "just play" without intentionality is a missed opportunity. She wants both — and she can tell in about 30 seconds on a website whether a school gets that balance right.

Her social work background gives her a particular sensitivity to how schools talk about children. She notices language. If a school's website says "behavior management," she hears discipline and control. If it says "regulation before expectation" or "behavior is a form of communication," she hears understanding and respect. The words on the website are not decoration to Megan — they're a signal of the school's culture.

Her son has delayed speech. He's in speech therapy and making progress, but Megan worries about how a school will handle a child who communicates differently. She won't ask about this on a first call. She'll look for signals on the website: Does the school mention inclusivity? Do they welcome children of all abilities? Is there any indication that a child who isn't "typical" would be embraced here, not merely tolerated?

She considered a faith-based preschool in East Peoria — it's closer, cheaper, and several friends send their kids there. But something about it felt generic. She wants a school with a point of view, led by someone who can explain why they do what they do. A school run by a doctoral researcher in early childhood education resonates with Megan not because she's impressed by degrees — but because it tells her that someone is thinking deeply about what matters for her son.

What Megan Is Looking For

  • A school whose philosophy she can articulate to her husband over dinner: "They believe play is how children learn, but every activity is designed with a purpose." She needs the words to explain the school to others.
  • Visible signals of inclusivity — not a page dedicated to special needs, but a natural, woven-in message that every child belongs regardless of ability, pace, or background
  • A warm, homelike environment where her son won't feel overwhelmed on his first day. The shoes-off culture, the small classes, the teachers who know every child's name.
  • A leader who doesn't just run the school but designed the curriculum from her own research. Megan trusts expertise when it's authentic, not performative.
  • A community she can see herself belonging to. Megan is driving from East Peoria — she needs to feel that the drive is worth it, that this is her people.

Megan's Dominant Emotional Triggers

TriggerWhat It Sounds Like in Megan's Head
Belonging (Primary)"Will my son be welcomed for who he is? Will we fit in here? Will the teachers see him — really see him?"
Trust"She has a doctorate. She's researched this. She didn't just open a school — she designed one based on evidence. That tells me she'll take my son's development seriously."
Guilt"He just turned 3. He's still in speech therapy. Am I pushing him into a world he's not ready for? Or am I giving him exactly the social exposure he needs right now?"

What Converts Megan

  • The "welcoming children of all abilities" language positioned naturally within program descriptions, not on a separate "special needs" page
  • Dr. Peterson's origin story on the About page — especially the softened personal connection to growing up alongside a family member with autism. This tells Megan that inclusivity isn't a marketing checkbox; it's personal.
  • Ms. Brenna's professional endorsement ("children who are often more socially engaged than in less accommodating environments") — this is the proof point Megan needs to convince herself AND her husband
  • Fresh 3's Fridays as a low-commitment entry point. Megan may not be ready to commit to T/Th at first. One day a week lets her son try the environment — and lets Megan see how the teachers interact with him.

Copy Implications for Megan

  • Blog content targeting "inclusive preschool," "preschool for children with speech delays," and "how play-based learning supports all learners" will capture Megan at the discovery stage
  • Photography on the website must reflect diversity — in ability, in ethnicity, in how children engage. Megan notices representation instantly.
  • The tour page should explicitly mention that parents are welcome to ask about how Spark supports individual learning differences. This gives Megan permission to ask the question she's been holding.
  • Location pages for East Peoria and the broader Peoria metro are critical — Megan isn't searching "Morton preschool" because she doesn't live there. She's searching her own city plus "preschool."
