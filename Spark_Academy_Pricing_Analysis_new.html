Executive Summary

The bottom line on Spark's pricing in the Peoria metro market

The Verdict: She's Underpriced for What She Offers

Dr. Peterson has built a genuinely premium product — doctoral-led, 5:1 ratios, proprietary curriculum, daily enrichment rotation — and is pricing it in the low-to-middle of the local market. Her M–F Kindergarten program at $7,000/year sits below the Illinois private preschool average of $11,275/year. The biggest gap isn't what she charges — it's the revenue she's not capturing through missing fee structures, no sibling discounts, no extended care, and a half-day-only model that excludes working parents entirely.

Key Findings at a Glance

💰
Premium product, mid-market pricing

Spark's enrichment stack (Spanish, STEM, Art, Communication, Music & Movement daily), 5:1 ratio, and doctoral leadership are objectively superior to every local competitor. Yet her 2-day programs start at just $350/mo — only $13.46/hr — which is competitive with, not premium above, the market.

⚠️
Half-day model limits addressable market

Every major local competitor (Cadence Academy, Apple Tree Academy, Faithful Heart, Childtime) offers full-day or extended care. Working parents who love Spark's philosophy still can't enroll if they need coverage past noon. This is the single biggest revenue constraint.

💡
Missing fee structures leave money on the table

No annual materials fee, no registration fee beyond $100 deposit, no sibling discount to capture multi-child families, and no extended care add-on. These are standard across competitors and represent low-risk revenue adds.

SWOT Analysis — Pricing Lens

Evaluating Spark's pricing power through strategic factors

🟢 Strengths

  • Unmatched credentials: Only Ed.D.-led preschool in Central IL. No competitor can replicate this.
  • 5:1 ratio: IL minimum for preschool is 1:10. Spark doubles the standard — a concrete, marketable differentiator.
  • Daily enrichment rotation: Spanish, STEM, Art, Communication, Music & Movement built into every day at no extra charge. Competitors charge separately for these or don't offer them at all.
  • Tuition transparency: Publishing prices builds trust and pre-qualifies serious families. Most competitors hide pricing behind "call for rates."
  • Proprietary curriculum (Ignite Learning Model): Named, documented methodology creates perceived value competitors can't match.
  • Multiple schedule tiers: T/Th, M/W/F, and M–F options let families scale commitment and cost. Smart pricing ladder.
  • Early-pay discount (10%): Incentivizes cash flow predictability for the business.

🔴 Weaknesses

  • Half-day only: 3-hour (3's/4's) and 4-hour (K-Prep/K) windows exclude dual-income families who need 8+ hours of coverage. This is the #1 market limitation.
  • No infant/toddler pipeline: Competitors like Cadence, Faithful Heart, and Apple Tree Academy capture families at 6 weeks–2 years. By the time those kids hit age 3, parents already have loyalty.
  • No sibling discount: Apple Tree Academy offers a 5% auto-pay discount. Cadence offers 10% off the eldest. Families with 2+ children are price-sensitive to total cost, not per-child cost.
  • No materials/activity fee: The $100 deposit is reasonable, but most competitors also charge $70–$300/year for supplies and curriculum materials.
  • Small capacity (~40–48 students): Limited revenue ceiling regardless of pricing. Every empty seat costs more.
  • New school (est. 2023): Limited track record. Zero Google reviews currently. Price premium requires trust that hasn't been fully established yet.

🔵 Opportunities

  • Extended care add-on ($150–$250/mo): Even 2 hours of afternoon care (noon–2pm) would capture working parents who can stagger shifts or pair with a sitter for the remaining hours.
  • Annual materials fee ($150–$200): Standard in the industry. Covers art supplies, curriculum materials, Developmental Play Room inventory. Pure margin.
  • Sibling discount (10% off 2nd child): Net revenue positive — capturing a 2-child family at 90% is better than losing them entirely to a competitor with a discount.
  • Camp Play (Confirmed for 2026): Fills the 2-month tuition gap (June–August), provides an entry point for families to "try" Spark before committing to fall enrollment, and generates an estimated $21,000 in summer revenue.
  • Moderate price increase (5–8% for 2027–28): Spark's current rates have headroom. A $25–$50/mo increase across programs with 60 days' notice is well within market tolerance, especially paired with a new website and expanded visibility.
  • Premium pricing tier: If extended care is added, a "full experience" bundle (AM program + PM extended care + Fresh 3's Fridays) could command $600–$800/mo for 3's, approaching what parents already pay for full-time daycare.

🟠 Threats

  • Free public preschool: Morton CUSD 709 offers free preschool for at-risk and IEP-identified children. Illinois Preschool for All may expand eligibility. This creates a free alternative for some families.
  • Full-day competitors: Cadence Academy (165-child capacity), Faithful Heart, and Childtime offer wrap-around care that Spark can't match. They compete on convenience, not quality.
  • Owner-operated premium competitors: Apple Tree Academy in Peoria offers full-day care with Creative Curriculum, soccer, and enrichment programs — at rates estimated around $1,200–$1,475/mo. Parents who want premium plus full-day coverage have alternatives.
  • Corporate chain marketing: Cadence and Childtime have national marketing budgets, live-streaming apps (SproutAbout), and employer partnership discounts. Hard to compete on visibility.
  • Economic sensitivity: Preschool is discretionary spending. A recession or significant cost-of-living increase could push families toward cheaper or free options.
  • Church-subsidized programs: Several area churches (including St. Mark's in Washington) offer preschool programs at significantly below-market rates — some as low as $110–$130/mo. These aren't direct competitors on quality, but they capture price-first families who might otherwise consider Spark.
  • Teacher labor costs: Maintaining 5:1 ratios means higher payroll-to-revenue ratio. If labor costs rise, margins compress faster at Spark than at competitors with 1:8 or 1:10 ratios.

SWOT Summary Assessment

Spark's strengths (credentials, curriculum, ratio) create genuine pricing power that isn't being fully exercised. The weaknesses (half-day only, no pipeline, small capacity) are structural and constrain revenue more than any pricing decision. The opportunities are actionable and low-risk — most could be implemented for the 2027–28 school year. The threats are real but largely affect different market segments: families who prioritize full-day convenience over educational quality will default to Cadence or Apple Tree regardless of Spark's price. Spark's real competitive set is premium-seeking parents who value Dr. Peterson's doctoral expertise and intimate class sizes but currently have to compromise on hours.

Gap Analysis

What she got right, what's missing, and where the money is

✅

What Dr. Peterson Got Right

Tiered scheduling creates a natural price ladder

The T/Th → M/W/F → M–F progression lets families self-select based on budget and commitment. This is textbook pricing architecture — it captures both price-sensitive families ($350) and invested families ($600–$700) without discounting the premium tier.

Tuition transparency is a conversion advantage

Research from the keyword analysis confirms "how much does preschool cost" and "preschool cost" are high-volume search terms (2,500+/mo combined). Publishing prices filters out unqualified leads and builds immediate trust with serious prospects — especially the "David & Priya" persona who will leave a site that hides pricing.

10% early-pay discount is well-calibrated

Industry standard is 3–5% for lump-sum payment. Spark's 10% is generous enough to motivate action (a $350–$840 savings depending on program) while securing predictable cash flow before the school year starts. The Aug 15 deadline is smart — it's before school starts but after enrollment decisions are made.

The enrichment stack is built in, not billed separately

Spanish, STEM, Art, Communication, Music & Movement every day, included in tuition. Many competitors charge $50–$150/mo extra for even one enrichment activity. This is a massive value proposition that should be prominently marketed on the website as "What's Included" rather than buried in program descriptions.

Kindergarten pricing creates a clear premium tier

At $700/mo (M–F only), the Kindergarten program signals seriousness and selectivity. The Sept 30 birthday cutoff and 4-hour day differentiate it from public kindergarten. Parents paying $700/mo for private kindergarten are buying confidence that their child is academically and socially ready — a powerful value proposition.

🔍

Where the Gaps Are

💰
No annual materials or curriculum fee

The Developmental Play Room changes monthly. Daily enrichment requires supplies. Art projects need materials. Every competitor charges $70–$300/year for this. At even $150/year across 40 students, that's $6,000 in margin Spark isn't capturing.

💰
No sibling discount

Apple Tree Academy offers a 5% auto-pay discount. Cadence offers 10% off the oldest child. A family with a 3-year-old and 4-year-old at Spark pays $700–$1,050/mo total. Even 10% off the 2nd child ($35–$60/mo discount) would make Spark more competitive for multi-child families — and it's net revenue positive vs. losing the family entirely.

⚠️
No extended care option (the biggest structural gap)

A 3-hour program ending at 11:30am serves a narrow market: stay-at-home parents and part-time workers. In 2026, 72% of mothers with children under 6 participate in the labor force. Extended care to 2pm or 3pm — even just 3 days/week — would open Spark to a fundamentally larger addressable market. This doesn't require becoming a daycare; it's an enrichment extension.

⚠️
No family pipeline (infants/toddlers)

Families choose childcare at birth and build loyalty by age 3. Cadence captures them at 6 weeks. Faithful Heart at 6 weeks. Spark has no touchpoint until age 3 — by which time families are already committed elsewhere. A parent-and-me class, mommy-and-me morning, or toddler open gym could create early relationships without adding full infant care.

💡
Fresh 3's Fridays pricing may be too low

At $100/mo for a Friday enrichment day, Fresh 3's Fridays is priced lower per-session ($23.08/session) than the base 3's program ($40.38/session for T/Th). If it's the same enrichment quality with the same ratio, it should be priced closer to $125–$150/mo. At $100, it reads as an afterthought rather than a premium add-on.

Pricing Recommendations

Specific, actionable changes ordered by impact and feasibility

1. Add Extended Care — The Biggest Revenue Unlock

High Impact

Offer an optional afternoon extension (11:30am–2:00pm for 3's/4's, 12:00–2:00pm for K-Prep/K) on enrolled days. Position it as "Afternoon Enrichment" — not daycare. Include lunch, outdoor play, and quiet activities. Price at $200–$275/mo for M–F, $125–$175 for 2–3 day schedules.

Revenue impact: If 50% of families (≈20 students) add extended care at an average of $200/mo, that's $4,000/mo or $40,000/year in new revenue. This single change could increase total revenue by 15–25%.

2. Implement an Annual Curriculum & Materials Fee

High Impact

Charge a one-time annual fee of $175–$225 per student to cover the Developmental Play Room materials (which change monthly), enrichment supplies, art materials, and curriculum resources. This is industry standard — Cadence charges $70–$300/year in curriculum fees, and parents expect it. Frame it as "Your child's Ignite Learning Kit" on the enrollment form.

Revenue impact: $200 × 40 students = $8,000/year in nearly pure margin. No operational change required.

3. Introduce a Sibling Discount (10% off 2nd child)

Medium Impact

Offer 10% off the younger child's tuition when two or more siblings are enrolled simultaneously. This matches the market standard (Apple Tree offers auto-pay discounts, Cadence offers 10% off the oldest child, and most local competitors offer some form of multi-child pricing). The math works in Spark's favor: capturing a 2-child family at 90% on the second child generates more revenue than losing them to a competitor that offers the discount.

Revenue impact: Net positive. Example: A family with a 3-year-old (T/Th at $350) and 4-year-old (M/W/F at $450) currently pays $800/mo. With 10% off the 3-year-old, they'd pay $765/mo. Without the discount, they might choose Cadence or Faithful Heart. Capturing the family at $765 vs. losing them entirely is a no-brainer.

4. Raise Fresh 3's Fridays to $125–$150/mo

Medium Impact

The current $100/mo price undervalues the offering. At $23/session vs. the base program's $40/session, it signals "extra" rather than "enrichment." Increase to $125–$150/mo to align per-session value with the core program and reflect the enrichment quality. If bundled with T/Th enrollment, consider $125. As a standalone Friday add-on, $150 is justified.

Revenue impact: If 10 students participate, a $25–$50/mo increase = $2,500–$5,000/year in additional revenue.

5. Plan a 5–8% Tuition Increase for 2027–28

Medium Impact

With the new website, stronger online presence, Google reviews, and a documented proprietary curriculum, Spark will have significantly more brand equity by early 2027. A modest across-the-board increase communicated 90 days in advance is market-appropriate and won't trigger attrition at this level. This means the 3's T/Th goes from $350 to $370–$380, K-Prep M–F from $650 to $685–$700, etc.

Revenue impact: A 6.5% average increase across 40 students at an average tuition of $475/mo = approximately $12,350/year in additional revenue.

6. Create an Early-Bird / Loyalty Re-Enrollment Incentive

Low Impact

Offer returning families a small incentive to re-enroll by a specific date (e.g., February 1): waived registration deposit, locked-in current-year rate for one more year, or priority scheduling. This reduces enrollment uncertainty, improves cash flow forecasting, and strengthens retention. The cost is minimal since returning families were likely to re-enroll anyway — the incentive just accelerates the decision and reduces administrative churn.
